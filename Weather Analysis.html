Introduction, Context and Short literature review on the problem

Windspeed and precipitation patterns are intricately tied to atmospheric dynamics. The Earths atmosphere is mainly driven by solar heating, with the equator receiving the most direct sunlight. This causes the air to warm and rise, creating a low-pressure zone, while cooler, denser air at the poles forms high-pressure areas. The rotation of the Earth causes air to accumulate around 30 latitude, creating high-pressure belts. On the other hand surface winds are influenced by friction with the Earths terrain, which alters their speed and direction, causing them to cross isobars and flow toward low-pressure zones.

Humidity plays a crucial role in weather and climate, influencing cloud formation, storm development, and precipitation. At 100% relative humidity, the air reaches its maximum moisture-holding capacity at a given temperature. However, this does not guarantee precipitation, as other factors like atmospheric pressure, temperature, and condensation nuclei are also essential for precipitation. Even when the air is saturated, conditions like fog can form without rain, and rainfall can occur even if surface humidity is below 100%.

Wind patterns and humidity are critical in understanding weather events such as storms and wind-driven disasters. Strong winds, particularly during winter windstorms, have significant implications for various sectors, including construction and transportation. The precision of wind speed measurements is vital for forecasting, and areas with frequent strong winds tend to have more resilient infrastructure absence of which can lead to higher costs of repairs, longer recovery times, and increased risks during future storms. In addition, changes in rainfall during dry seasons can lead to soil erosion and agricultural productivity leading to economic strain which highlights the importance of understanding and predicting these complex atmospheric interactions.

Data Sources Presentation

For this project, the primary data source is Meteoblue, accessed via an API that provides weather forecasts and current data, including temperature, wind speed, precipitation, and humidity. Meteoblues weather models simulate physical atmospheric processes by dividing regions into small grid cells, each approximately 4 km to 40 km wide and 100 m to 2 km high. These models feature 60 vertical atmospheric layers, reaching up to 60 km into the stratosphere. Weather is simulated every few seconds by solving complex mathematical equations between the grid cells, storing weather variables like temperature, wind speed, and cloudiness every hour. Meteoblue operates proprietary models based on NMM (Nonhydrostatic Meso-Scale Modelling) technology and integrates open data from various sources. The models are run twice daily on a dedicated High Performance Cluster, generating forecasts for large domains covering parts of continents. These domains are embedded within global models, providing boundary conditions for the forecasts. Each grid cell considers specific factors like position, altitude, and land surface type, ensuring high-quality weather forecasting for locations ranging from city centers to remote mountain areas. I used Meteoblues free package to fetch data for the United Kingdom, and the geographic data was sourced from a shapefile provided by the Office for National Statistics. The data was processed using Geopandas and Matplotlib, allowing for the creation of static maps to visualize regional weather patterns.

# Function to determine marker color based on temperature
def get_marker_color(temp):
    if temp < 5:
        return "darkblue"  
    elif 5 <= temp < 7:
        return "yellow"  
    elif 7 <= temp < 10:
        return "orange"  
    else:
        return "red"  

# Function to determine marker icon based on temperature
def get_marker_icon(temp):
    if temp < 5:
        return "snowflake"  
    elif 5 <= temp < 7:
        return "cloud"  
    elif 7 <= temp < 10:
        return "cloud-sun"  
    else:
        return "sun"  

# Function to add smaller markers to a folium map
def add_markers_to_map(df, folium_map):
    for index, row in df.iterrows():
        temp = row['temperature']
        marker_color = get_marker_color(temp)
        marker_icon = get_marker_icon(temp)

        # Using CircleMarker with a slightly smaller radius
        CircleMarker(
            location=[row['lat'], row['lon']],
            radius=4,  
            color=marker_color,
            fill=True,
            fill_color=marker_color,
            fill_opacity=0.7,
            popup=f"City: {row['city']}<br>Temperature: {temp}C"
        ).add_to(folium_map)

        # Adding an icon on top of the smaller marker
        folium.Marker(
            location=[row['lat'], row['lon']],
            icon=Icon(color=marker_color, icon=marker_icon, prefix='fa'),
        ).add_to(folium_map)

The first static map shows the variation of wind speed all over United Kingdom. Mostly we can see that the high wind speed is near the coastal regions which are more exposed to the sea. In the second static map we see that humidity is also high in these regions because the ocean currents in North Atlantic also adds the moisture to these coastal areas. The area specifically with strong winds and high humidity being Coastal regions of northern Scotland, The coastlines in Wales and Isles of Scilly and Isle of Man. The Clausius-Clapeyron relationship suggests that for every 1C rise in temperature, extreme precipitation may increase by approximately 6.5%. This makes temperature forecasts a useful tool for predicting heavy rainfall and taking proactive measures to reduce potential damage. In simple terms, as temperatures rise, the atmosphere can hold more moisture, leading to increased rainfall.
